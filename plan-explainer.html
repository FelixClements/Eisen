V1 architecture & delivery plan

A stakeholder guide

Your tasks. Your device. Your control.

Eisen V1 is a native task app built around the Eisenhower Matrix. It works offline first, keeps task content encrypted, and makes cloud sync a deliberate option—not a condition of using the product.

A plan, not a shipped product

The repository starts without an app or sync service. The first work is to freeze, test, and review the safety rules before product polish or cloud delivery begins.

01 / Product promise

A useful task app before the internet arrives.

The starting point is a personal, local vault. A person can create and use it without an account, network connection, or sync provider. Eisen organizes tasks in the four urgent × important quadrants, then adds optional ways to move the same encrypted history between devices.

“Local-first” means a new task is saved safely on the device first. A poor connection, service outage, or closed laptop does not stop the work.

01

Stay productive offline

Create, edit, complete, restore, delete, and move tasks with no account required.

02

Choose the right reach

Keep a vault on one device, sync it through encrypted cloud delivery, or use a live peer relay.

03

Keep a recoverable record

Changes are kept as a durable history, with explicit encrypted export and recovery-package flows.

Included in V1

  • Native Windows and Android apps
  • Four-quadrant task management and completed/deleted views
  • Encrypted local vault, history, and task view
  • Optional enrollment, cloud delivery, and live relay
  • Encrypted backup/export, recovery, and device revocation flows

Deliberately not V1

  • macOS, web, shared vaults, and per-task permissions
  • Server-side task search, analytics, conflict decisions, or plaintext notifications
  • Automatic recovery when all user-held secrets are lost
  • A promise that Android background sync works on every device configuration
  • Anonymous networking or hidden service/network metadata

02 / Transport choices

One vault, three delivery states.

The cloud and relay are couriers, not a master copy. They carry the same encrypted changes. Each device checks them and resolves any overlap locally, so turning a transport on or off never deletes the person’s local work.

Default

Local only

The vault stays on the device. The person can move it only by deliberately exporting and importing an encrypted recovery package.

Trade-off: no automatic multi-device sync. Recovery depends on the package and its user-held passphrase.

Optional

Encrypted cloud sync

Devices upload and download encrypted, immutable changes through an opaque delivery store. Devices can catch up at different times.

Trade-off: the service improves asynchronous delivery but can be unavailable, delay delivery, or retain only according to its stated policy.

Optional

Volatile relay

Devices that are online together exchange the same encrypted changes through a non-persistent relay.

Trade-off: a peer must be present. Missed changes are not held by the relay, so it is never presented as a backup.

03 / Local-first architecture

Plaintext lives on the device, not in the transport.

Each client has an encrypted local store and makes its own decisions. The network sees an opaque, authenticated package of change—not a task title, note, quadrant, or plaintext snapshot.

04 / Privacy, honestly stated

Strong content confidentiality—without overpromising.

The plan protects task content against a curious or breached transport and against altered or replayed traffic. It also names the limits clearly: encryption is not anonymity, availability, or protection from a compromised unlocked device.

What the design protects

  • Task content, task data, change contents, and snapshots are encrypted before they leave a device.
  • Every package is authenticated and signed by its source device; changed or unauthorized bytes are rejected.
  • Repeated delivery is safe: the same change applies once, and conflicting bytes under one change ID are treated as an integrity failure.
  • A received change is not acknowledged until it has been saved durably on the receiving device.

What it cannot promise

  • Cloud and relay operators can still see routing IDs, timing, sizes, approximate activity, IP/network data, and connection presence.
  • An outage or malicious service can delay, drop, withhold, or delete deliveries. A relay does not store missed work.
  • Malware, an unlocked session, screenshots, or weak device secure storage can expose what the device can read.
  • If every device and every recovery secret are lost, nobody—including Eisen support—can decrypt the existing vault.

Cryptographic building blocks are to come from maintained, platform-reviewed APIs and libraries. The plan explicitly avoids hand-built cryptographic primitives and requires independent review before public sync release.

05 / Devices, keys & data flow

A device earns trust; an account does not hold the vault.

A vault is the encryption and sync boundary. An optional account helps a service manage access and quotas, but never receives the vault key in plaintext. Each device has its own identity and must be enrolled by the vault owner.

Three distinct responsibilities

Owner signing keyThe V1 root of trust. It authorizes membership changes, revocation, and ownership transfer.
Device identity keysEach device has separate signing and encryption key pairs in platform secure storage.
Epoch vault keyA random key encrypts a period of vault history. A new random key is made when access is rotated.

Adding a device is an intentional, human-confirmed action. The owner device and new device use an authenticated QR-assisted flow, show a matching confirmation code, and exchange the access material over an encrypted channel. A QR code alone is never enough to add a device.

A user may also choose a recovery passphrase. It unlocks a client-created, encrypted recovery package; it is not a server-known password and cannot be reset by support.

  1. Save locally firstA task action reserves a safe encryption nonce, creates an encrypted immutable change, updates the local task view, and enters the durable outbox in one transaction.
  2. Send an opaque packageWhen a transport is enabled, the device authenticates to it and uploads changes. Safe retries use the same operation ID and digest.
  3. Receive in bounded batchesAnother device fetches changes through a cloud cursor or asks a live peer for missing ranges. Those two tracking methods are deliberately separate.
  4. Verify before openingThe recipient checks structure, version, signed membership, device signature, nonce domain, and encryption authentication before applying anything.
  5. Commit, then acknowledgeThe new change and receive checkpoint are saved together. Only then does the device acknowledge delivery; “acknowledged” means locally committed, not merely shown on screen.
Revocation needs a key rotation to become effective.

Removing a device is advisory while it still has an old key. The owner distributes a fresh epoch key to remaining devices and sets a cutover. After that point, old-epoch changes—even legitimate offline changes not seen before—are rejected. Revocation cannot erase ciphertext or plaintext a removed device already copied.

06 / Predictable convergence

Offline edits meet with rules, not guesswork.

Devices may edit while disconnected and receive changes late, more than once, or out of order. Eisen keeps an immutable log and has every client apply the same deterministic rules, so valid replicas converge on the same task view.

Field-level winners

Each mutable task field carries its own version. A change to a title should not unnecessarily replace a completion change made on another device. Versions use a persisted Hybrid Logical Clock plus a stable device-ID tie-breaker—not wall-clock display time.

Deletes remain visible to the protocol

Delete creates a tombstone rather than quietly erasing history. An older edit cannot bring the task back. Only an explicit newer restore can make it visible again. Conservative retention and signed snapshots help a long-offline device catch up safely.

Important UX implication: deterministic resolution is not the same as preventing every human conflict. The app must show enough history or change evidence to explain when a concurrent last-write-wins edit took precedence.
Why preserve a log instead of just syncing the current task list?

The history makes retries and duplicate delivery safe, supports reconciliation between devices, and gives clients evidence for repair. Snapshots can make bootstrap faster, but are signed optimizations—not an authority allowed to silently replace newer local work.

What happens when sync is inconsistent or data is missing?

The client preserves the existing vault, marks sync as needing repair, obtains a compatible encrypted snapshot, replays available later changes, and surfaces any unrecoverable discrepancy. It must never silently overwrite local data with a newer-looking server result.

07 / Platform behavior

Native clients that expect interruption.

The plan is intentionally Windows and Android first. It treats suspend, restart, loss of network, and mobile process death as ordinary conditions—not exceptional paths that can leave a vault half-saved.

W

Windows

Use a transactional local database and OS-supported secure key protection. Foreground sync is expected; background work uses supported Windows lifecycle mechanisms and resumes safely after suspension or launch.

  • Keep database, journals, snapshots, exports, logs, and crash artifacts inside the user-data protection boundary.
  • Persist work before the OS can suspend the app.
A

Android

Use a transactional database with Android Keystore-backed protection where available. Scheduled work follows platform constraints; the design never depends on a permanently running socket.

  • Handle Doze, network changes, vendor task killing, and process death by reconnecting and reconciling.
  • Respect lock state and offer a recovery path if protected key material becomes unavailable.
On both platforms: sensitive database files, journals, indexes, staged exports, diagnostics, and backups need explicit protection. Secure storage protects root or wrapping material; it does not automatically encrypt every database page. Plaintext should also stay out of notifications, clipboard history, analytics, crash reports, and app-switcher previews where platform controls permit.

08 / Release path

Prove the core before adding more ways to connect.

The delivery order is designed to reduce risk. The first durable cloud release waits for local storage, common protocol behavior, repair, and recovery to be proven. Live relay is deliberately last because it adds another failure surface without improving durable recovery.

Release slices

Each slice retains the plan’s security requirements.

Protocol laboratory

No public app. Freeze the schema, reference merge model, encryption-package test vectors, fault harness, and CI. Windows and Android must independently verify the same vectors.

Local-only product

Ship the smallest useful native task experience: encrypted vault, task actions, export/import, recovery package, repair flow, and honest local-only status.

Cloud-sync beta

Add accounts, owner-controlled enrollment, opaque cloud append/read delivery, durable outbox, snapshots, full resync, quotas, and support runbooks.

Cloud hardening

Use staged rollout, migration and rollback rehearsals, restore and retention exercises, independent review, fuzz/load testing, and lifecycle testing.

Volatile relay

Only after cloud reconciliation is stable. Demonstrate that the relay persists no operation payload outside bounded, active-session memory.

The implementation phases underneath this release order are: 0 specify; 1 local crypto and merge core; 2 functional native task experience; 3 enrollment and cloud delivery; 4 relay and safe switching; 5 hardening and release. UI polish is intentionally not allowed to outrun protocol correctness.

What has to be specified before feature work starts?

Before building, the team must close key decisions with Architecture Decision Records and executable evidence: native stacks and minimum OS support; canonical byte encoding; reviewed crypto APIs; owner-key custody; key rotation; task merge behavior; local encryption; counter rollback; snapshot acceptance; cloud consistency; recovery boundary; and the relay launch bar. The protocol also needs concrete contracts for envelopes, enrollment, recovery packages, cloud and relay APIs, errors, and compatibility.

09 / Decisions & risks

The hard choices are explicit.

This is not a plan to “figure out security later.” The choices that shape safety must be decided, recorded, tested, and reviewed before the affected work begins.

Native stack & lifecycle

Select supported Windows and Android stacks, secure storage, background APIs, and release/signing approach from spike evidence.

Canonical bytes

Choose a deterministic, bounded format with duplicate-field rejection so every client signs and verifies identical data.

Reviewed crypto suite

Approve encryption, key derivation, passphrase protection, digest, signing, and authenticated exchange through maintained libraries.

Owner custody & rotation

Keep a separate owner key, one V1 management device, and fresh random epoch keys that never derive from a revoked key.

Merge, storage & rollback

Freeze field-level merge and tombstone rules; encrypt all disk-backed derivatives; fail closed if a nonce counter may have rolled back.

Snapshots, cloud & recovery

Define signed snapshot coverage, cloud receipt/cursor semantics, and a user-held recovery package with no server passphrase reset.

10 / Definition of done

“Built” means demonstrably safe, recoverable, and explainable.

A feature is not complete because a happy-path demo works. The plan requires evidence that the behavior is specified, survives failure, protects secrets, and has a clear user-facing outcome.

Every protocol or storage task must show

  • A versioned specification plus at least one valid and one invalid test vector
  • Agreement between the reference model and both client implementations on bytes and resulting state
  • Tests for retries, restart, duplicates, reordering, partial writes, and corruption where relevant
  • No secrets in logs, telemetry, crash reports, fixtures, or committed files
  • Limits checked before costly parsing, allocation, decryption, or database writes
  • Resumable, tested migrations and documentation updated in the same change
  • Failure screens that say what remains safe, what the person can do, and what cannot be guaranteed

Release gates in plain terms

Gate 0 — Freeze the rulesDecisions, schemas, vectors, and the local-first release order are approved.
Gate 1–2 — Prove local correctnessBoth platforms survive failures and converge after offline, delayed, duplicated, or reordered changes.
Gate 3 — Prove cloud betaThe service cannot read task content or authorize a forged change; repair and full resync are tested.
Gate 4 — Prove relay boundariesNo payload persistence outside active-session memory; resource limits and dual transport are safe.
Gate 5 — Earn public releaseIndependent review, migration/lifecycle/load/fuzz/recovery tests, signed builds, operations, privacy, support, and staged rollout are ready.
